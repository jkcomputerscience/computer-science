Types of Networks

LAN:

  • Local Area Network.
  • All devices are connected onto ONE network via a switch.
  • No routers are involved.
  • Usually only has one owner.
  • For example: Homes, schools etc.

WAN:

  • Wide Area Network.
  • Several networks are connected via routers.
  • Both switches and routers are involved.
  • Multiple owners.
  • For example: the internet.

Transmission Mediums: Wired

A good transmission medium would lose no data when one device sends another device a message.

There are two common types of wired transmission mediums:

  • A twisted pair.
  • optic.

Twisted pair:

  • A twisted pair uses an electrical signal.
  • Consists of 2 copper wires twisted around each other.
  • One signal goes down one wire and the other down the other wire.

Advantages:

  • Inexpensive.
  • Flexible.
  • Secure.
  • Access is controlled.

Disadvantage:

  • Limited range - about 90m.

Fibre Optic:

  • A fibre optic uses light signals.
  • Made of little transparent rods.

Advantages:

  • Fast.
  • Very long range - about 10,000m.
  • Secure.
  • Access is controlled.

Disdvantage:

  • Expensive.

Transmission Mediums: Wireless

A good transmission medium would lose no data when one device sends another device a message.

Description:

  • Signals pass as radio signals.
  • Also known as WiFi.
  • Devices must have wirless capability, and a wireless acess point.
  • Wireless capability could be a USB dongole or a HDMI cable.

Advantages:

  • Flexible - can be extended.
  • Low cost.
  • Fast because radio waves are fast.

Disadvantages:

  • Less secure - If someone knows the password you can get in.
  • Signals can easily be blocked by large, heavy objects.
